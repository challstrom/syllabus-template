    Section 2 Course Content and Objectives

    This course is an introduction to differential and integral calculus with an emphasis on applications in the natural and physical sciences. It is aimed at mathematics, engineering, and science majors or anyone else interested in building a foundation for further study in mathematics. Course-level learning goals include: communicating effectively about course content and solution methods; per- sistence in solving challenging, multi-step problems; reflection on one?s own learning; effective use of technology. Certain affective traits (i.e., dispositions and habits that are useful for all learners, regardless of topic) are also valued, such as persistence and growth mindset, and the development of these qualities will be fostered by the course.

    Subsection 2.1 University Core

    Calculus at the University of Portland is a part of our liberal arts Core Curriculum with a vision of students acquiring knowledge, skills, and values that will prepare you to respond to the needs of the world and its human family, while having a foundation of multiple lenses to address enduring questions of human concern. The Core Curriculum strives to achieve this vision by cultivating six habits of heart and mind in all graduates regardless of major.
    As a priority for this Core class, our focus is the “scientific and quantitative literacy and problem solving” habit. As part of developing that habit this course will prioritize students learning to employ mathematical and statistical skills to explore and make sense of data. Toward this end, as one specific Core learning outcome for this class, the expectation is that students who complete this course will be able to use the tools of differential calculus to solve complex problems and effectively communicate solutions in written form.

    Subsection 2.2 Learning Objectives

    By the end of the course, you should be able to say the following (items marked with (*) are considered core objectives).
    Limits
    1. (*) I can explain the meaning of a limit beyond describing how to calculuate it.
    2. (*) I can find one and two sided limits of a functino at a point and at inifinity using numerical, graphical, and algebraic methods.
    3. I can determine the points at which a function is (or is not) continuous and can use continuity to determine limits.
    4. I can identify limits in indeterminate form and can apply L’Hopital’s rule correctly.
    Derivatives
    1. (*) I can correcly interpret the meaning of a derivative as an instantaneous rate of change and compare it to an average rate of change both graphically and numerically.
    2. I can find the derivative of a function (both at a point and as a function) using the limit definition of the derivative.
    3. I can interpret teh meaning of a derivative and it’s units in a variety of contexts.
    4. (*) I can compute derivatives of polynomial and exponential functions.
    5. (*) I can compute basic derivates using the product, quotient, and chain rules.
    6. I can compute derivates of logarithmic, trigonometric, and inverse trig functions.
    7. I can use techniques of implicit and logarithmic differentiation.
    8. (*) I can compute derivates using combinations of multiple rules and strategies.
    Applications
    1. (*) I can find the equation of the tangent line to a graph of a function at a point. I can interpret the linear approximation graphically and use it to estimate function values and error.
    2. (*) I can use information about a function and its derivatives to describe its behavior, including its concavity and the shape of its graph.
    3. (*) I can use derivates to solve optimization problems.
    4. I can use derivatives to solve related rate problems.
    Integrals
    1. I can calculate the area between curves, net change, and displacement using geometric methods, including Riemann sums.
    2. I can use properities and theorems to calculate and interpret definte integrals.
    3. (*) I can explain the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus in terms of net change and displacements in graphical and applied contexts.
    4. I can find antiderivates of a function and use them to evaluate definite integrals using the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus.
    Connections
    1. I can effectively communicate understand of course content using words, graphs, and notation.
    2. I can demonstrate perseverance in solving complex and unfamiliar problems.
    3. (*) I can evaluate the validity of statements and construct examples (or counterexamples) illustrating major concepts.
    4. I can demonstrate a habit of consistent practice.
    5. I can reflect on my own learning practices and use that reflection to grow as a learner.
